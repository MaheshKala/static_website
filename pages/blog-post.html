It is a non-selective, pure agonist at mu, delta, and kappa opioid receptors with a higher affinity for the mu receptor. Other mechanisms which may contribute to its analgesic effect are inhibition of neuronal reuptake of noradrenaline and enhancement of serotonin release.

In animal experiments, it has been shown that tramadol is a weak inhibitor of the neuronal reuptake of noradrenaline and serotonin. These mechanisms may contribute to the analgesic action of tramadol. Tramadol has an antitussive effect. In contrast to morphine, analgesic doses of tramadol over a wide range have no respiratory depressant effect. Also, gastrointestinal motility is not affected. Its effects on the cardiovascular system tend to be slight. The potency of tramadol is reported to be 1/10 to 1/6 of that of morphine.

The pharmacokinetics of tramadol have been investigated following oral, rectal, intravenous, and intramuscular administration in healthy volunteers. After oral administration of tramadol, the mean absolute bioavailability is approximately 70%. The mean peak plasma concentration (Cmax) of 309 ng/mL is achieved at 2 h after a single 100 mg oral dose of tramadol tablets. The mean Cmax of O-desmethyltramadol (M1), the main active metabolite, is 55 ng/mL at 3 h.
